About Ashley

It's nice to meet you!

I'm running for County Commission in District 3 because I believe strong communities are built by people who are rooted in the place they call home — and who care for it in both big and small ways.

Born and raised from East Tennessee. I chose to build my life in Jonesborough, upon graduating from ETSU in 2015, I found work with Jonesborough Locally Grown's Boone Street Market. It's here in Washington County that my values feel most alive.This isn't just where I live — it's where I've invested my time, my energy, and my heart & home.

Professionally, I work as the Director of Food Access with the Appalachian Resource Conservation & Development Council, supporting conservation, local agriculture, and community-based initiatives across the region. Every day, I see how open spaces, infrastructure, water quality, and food systems intersect — and how local government decisions shape the long-term health of our communities.

I currently serve as Chair of Keep Jonesborough Beautiful, where I work alongside volunteers to protect and enhance the place we call home. I'm also involved with Slow Food Tri-Cities, strengthening connections between local farmers, food traditions, and community health.

My commitment to thoughtful planning extends beyond my day-to-day work. I served on the advisory board for Johnson City Horizon 2045, helping shape a long-term vision for responsible growth. I've also served as an advisor for Unity Housing, supporting efforts to expand access to stable, affordable housing.

Food access and dignity have always been central to my work. During my time with One Acre Cafe, a community café serving meals with dignity regardless of ability to pay, I witnessed firsthand how powerful a shared meal can be. It reinforced my belief that public service should center humanity, respect, and practical solutions to food insecurity.

Community building is something I believe in deeply. Over the years, I've volunteered and served on planning committees for festivals and events across the Tri-Cities, including the Meet the Mountains Festival, Blue Plum Festival, and Jonesborough Farm to Table Dinner. These gatherings celebrate our culture, support local businesses, and remind us how connected we are to one another.

Outside of work and service, you'll most often find me in the garden, on a trail, or near the water. Gardening keeps me grounded and reinforces my belief in stewardship and patience. Hiking through Northeast Tennessee reminds me why smart land use planning matters. And I've always had a deep love for rivers and water — whether walking a streambank, paddling, or simply sitting by the water. Our waterways are part of our identity and our responsibility.

Food and cooking are another way I connect with others. I love preparing meals with local ingredients and gathering friends and neighbors around the table. Food is Appalachian culture, history, and community — and ensuring food security here at home is something I care deeply about.

Travel has broadened my perspective and deepened my appreciation for different cultures and traditions. But every time I return home to Jonesborough, I'm reminded why I chose to plant roots here.

I also believe that civic engagement doesn't stop at meetings and boards. I have stood alongside neighbors at protests and gatherings advocating for equal rights and fairness, because I believe everyone deserves dignity, opportunity, and a voice. Showing up matters — whether at a public hearing, a community event, or in peaceful advocacy for justice.

And when I'm not working or volunteering, I'm usually with my Jack Russell, Banjo — my constant sidekick, new campaign manager, and best friend. Whether we're hiking, gardening (he helps dig the holes), or out for a walk, Banjo reminds me to play, laugh, and appreciate the everyday beauty of this place.

All of these experiences — my work, board service, planning initiatives, gardening, food systems, festivals, rivers, and community involvement — shape how I approach leadership. They remind me that County Commission decisions affect our land, our water, our housing, our local economy, and the quality of life we pass on to the next generation.

I'm running for District 3 because I don't just live here — I serve here. And I believe leadership should reflect the same care, stewardship, and long-term thinking we bring to our gardens, our waterways, and our neighborhoods.
